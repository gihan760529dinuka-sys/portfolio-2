1. Contract Law (Simple English)

What is a contract?

A contract is a promise that the law will enforce. For a promise to become a contract, 5 things are needed:

  1. Offer: One person makes a clear proposal to another.
  2. Acceptance: The other person says “yes” exactly as offered.
  3. Consideration (Justa Causa): Something of value moves between the parties (money, work, goods).
  4. Intention to create legal relations: Both sides must want the law to step in if someone breaks the promise.
  5. Legal form: Most contracts can be spoken or written, but a few must be in writing by law.

Offer rules

  • Can be made to one person, a group, or the whole world (example: reward advert).
  • Must be communicated to the offeree.
  • Ends if it is revoked (taken back), rejected, or a time limit passes.

Acceptance rules

  • Must be an exact “mirror” of the offer (no new conditions).
  • Silence is not acceptance.
  • Usually must reach the offeror, except:
    • Post rule: if post is the expected way, acceptance is complete as soon as the letter is posted.
    • Unilateral contracts: doing the act (like using the smoke ball) is enough; no need to tell anyone.

Invitation to treat (not an offer)

  • Goods on shop shelves, adverts, auction ads = invitations to start talks, not firm offers.

Terms of the contract

  • Express terms: actually spoken or written.
  • Implied terms: added by law, custom or court to make the contract work.
  • Conditions: vital terms. If broken, the innocent side can end the contract and claim damages.
  • Warranties: smaller terms. Only damages can be claimed.

Ending a contract

  • By full performance (job done).
  • By agreement (both sides agree to stop).
  • By breach of a condition.
  • By frustration (something impossible happens, e.g. goods destroyed by flood).

Remedies if someone breaks the contract

  1. Damages (money).
  2. End the contract.
  3. Specific performance (court order to do what was promised).
  4. Injunction (court order to stop doing something).
  5. Payment for work already done.

2. Law of Agency (Simple English)

What is agency?

One person (the agent) is allowed to make contracts for another person (the principal). The contract is between the principal and the third party, not the agent.

How agency is created

  1. Agreement: spoken or written (e.g. power of attorney).
  2. Ratification: principal later approves an act done without authority.
  3. Necessity: emergency forces someone to act for another.
  4. Estoppel: principal’s behaviour makes others believe someone is their agent.

Duties of an agent

  • Obey instructions.
  • Act with care and skill.
  • Do the job personally (no delegation unless allowed).
  • Avoid conflict of interest (cannot secretly buy the goods he is asked to sell).
  • No secret profits or bribes.

Rights of an agent

  • Be paid the agreed commission.
  • Be repaid for money spent or losses made while doing the job.

Types of authority

  • Express: clearly given by words or writing.
  • Implied: needed to do the main job (e.g. buy stamps to post a letter).
  • Apparent/Ostensible: principal’s behaviour makes outsiders think the agent has power, even if he does not.

Ending an agency

  • Job finished or time expired.
  • Either side says “I quit” or “You’re fired”.
  • Death, bankruptcy or insanity of either side.
  • Frustration (e.g. subject matter destroyed).

3. Sale of Goods (Simple English)

What is a sale of goods?

A contract where the seller transfers or agrees to transfer ownership of movable goods to the buyer for a money price.

Types of goods

  • Existing goods: already owned by seller.
    • Specific: identified and agreed (this car).
    • Unascertained: only described (30 bags of rice).
  • Future goods: not yet made or acquired by seller.

When does ownership (property) pass?

Rule of thumb: loss follows ownership. Five rules decide the exact moment:

  1. Specific goods in deliverable state: at contract time.
  2. Specific goods needing something done: when done and buyer told.
  3. Goods to be weighed/tested: after testing and buyer told.
  4. “Sale or return”: when buyer approves or keeps goods beyond reasonable time.
  5. Unascertained/future goods: when goods are clearly set aside for this contract with both sides agreeing.

Implied promises (terms)

  • Seller has right to sell (condition).
  • Buyer will enjoy quiet possession (warranty).
  • Goods are free from hidden charges (warranty).
  • Goods match the description (condition).
  • Goods are of satisfactory quality and fit for buyer’s purpose (conditions, unless defect was obvious or buyer examined and missed it).
  • Sample sales: bulk matches sample, no hidden defects.

Nemo dat rule & exceptions

“You can’t give what you don’t own.” Exceptions:

  • Mercantile agent (factor) selling goods entrusted to him.
  • Sale under a court order or statute.
  • Estoppel: owner acted like seller had authority.
  • Seller remains in possession and sells to second buyer who takes in good faith.

Unpaid seller’s rights

  • Against goods:
    • Lien (keep goods till paid).
    • Stoppage in transit (stop delivery if buyer insolvent).
    • Resell if perishable or notice given.
  • Against buyer: sue for price or damages.

Buyer’s remedies

  • Sue for damages for non-delivery.
  • Reject goods and end contract if condition broken.
  • Claim damages for any breach.
  • Ask court for specific performance (rare).

4. Sri Lanka Court System (Simple Guide)

Two big families of law

Civil LawCriminal Law
Goal: compensate the victimGoal: punish the offender
Case started by injured personCase started by State
Proof: balance of probabilityProof: beyond reasonable doubt

Personal laws (family matters only)

  • Thesawalamai – Jaffna Tamils
  • Kandyan Law – Kandyan Sinhalese
  • Muslim Law – followers of Islam

Common law for everything else

Roman-Dutch Law (RDL) is the main common law. English law fills gaps where RDL is silent.

Court levels

  1. Magistrate’s Court
    • Criminal: minor offences (fine < Rs 200 000 or jail < 2 years).
    • Civil: claims up to Rs 1 500.
  2. District Court
    • All civil cases (land, divorce, debt, damages) except big commercial cases.
    • Serious criminal cases (jury trials).
    • Hears appeals from Magistrate.
  3. High Court
    • Provincial High Court: appeals from District Court in serious crime.
    • Civil Appellate High Court: appeals in civil matters.
    • Commercial High Court: commercial disputes over Rs 20 million, company law, IP cases inside Western Province.
  4. Court of Appeal
    • Appeals from all High Courts.
    • Writ jurisdiction (orders to stop illegal acts, produce prisoners, force public officers to act).
    • Election petitions.
  5. Supreme Court
    • Final appeal court.
    • Interprets Constitution.
    • Fundamental-rights petitions.
    • Presidential election petitions.
    • Parliamentary privilege cases.

5. Negotiable Instruments (Simple English)

What does “negotiable instrument” mean?

A signed paper that promises or orders payment of money and can be passed from one person to another by simple delivery or endorsement.

Main features

  1. Easy transfer (like cash).
  2. Holder can sue in his own name.
  3. A person who takes it in good faith and for value (holder in due course) gets better title than the person who gave it.

Three big types

  • Bill of Exchange: written order by one person to another to pay money.
  • Cheque: bill of exchange drawn on a bank, payable on demand.
  • Promissory Note: written promise to pay money.

Parties you will meet

  • Drawer: person who signs the order (writes the cheque/bill).
  • Drawee/Acceptor: person who must pay (bank for cheque; anyone for bill).
  • Payee: person named to receive the money.
  • Endorser: person who signs on the back to pass it on.
  • Endorsee: person who receives it after endorsement.
  • Holder in due course: took the paper complete, on time, in good faith, paid value, no notice of defects – gets super-protected rights.

Cheque crossing (lines on cheque)

  • General crossing: two lines – cheque must be paid through any bank account.
  • Special crossing: bank name inside lines – paid only through that named bank.
  • Account-payee: words “A/C payee” – money must go into payee’s own account.
  • Not-negotiable: words “not negotiable” – transferee cannot become holder in due course; gets same title as transferor.

Presentment

  • Bills can be presented for acceptance (will you pay?) and for payment.
  • Cheques are only presented for payment (no acceptance step).

Dishonour

If a bill or cheque is not accepted or not paid, it is dishonoured. The holder must give notice of dishonour to earlier parties or lose the right to claim from them.

Endorsement styles

  • Blank: just signature – becomes payable to bearer.
  • Special: sign and name the next person – payable to that person.
  • Restrictive: “Pay Ali only” – cannot be passed further.
  • Conditional: “Pay Ben after ship arrives” – endorser liable only if condition happens, but endorsee can still sue others.
  • Sans recourse: endorser adds “sans recourse” – not liable if instrument bounces.

Discharge of the instrument

Rights end when:

  • Paid in due course.
  • Holder cancels it.
  • Holder gives up rights (renunciation).
  • Important details (date, amount, name) are fraudulently changed (material alteration).
